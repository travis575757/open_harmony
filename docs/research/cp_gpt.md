
Rules of Counterpoint and Voice Leading from Species to Common Practice
Executive Summary

Species counterpoint is a staged pedagogy that isolates one compositional variable at a time—first pure consonant alignment (Species 1), then controlled weak-beat dissonance (Species 2–3), then suspension-based syncopation (Species 4), and finally a composite “near-music” texture (Species 5).

Across the full arc from strict (modal) counterpoint to tonal, common-practice part writing, three invariants govern nearly all “rules”: (1) independence of lines (especially avoiding the perceptual fusion caused by parallel perfect consonances and excessive similar motion), (2) consonance/dissonance control (dissonance must be justified by a recognized linear pattern and resolved appropriately), and (3) cadence logic (endings are disproportionately rule-bound because they concentrate structural function).
Strict species, associated with the pedagogy of Johann Joseph Fux and his Gradus ad Parnassum, treats consonance categories and motion types as primary, and treats harmonic implication as a byproduct of correct line-writing. In contrast, common-practice voice leading (as in standard harmony/part-writing curricula) treats harmony and counterpoint as mutually constraining: chord function and tendency tones strongly determine what voice-leading is acceptable, and “good counterpoint” is often evaluated in chordal terms (sevenths resolve down, leading tones resolve up, cadential 6/4 behaves as a dominant embellishment, etc.).

Where “later pedagogical expansions” diverge from strict Fux-style species, they typically (a) broaden the inventory of idiomatic dissonances (e.g., neighbor tones, cambiatas, accented dissonances in controlled contexts), (b) loosen the one-pattern-per-species purity in favor of musically realistic mixtures earlier, and (c) incorporate style-specific constraints (Renaissance text-driven phrase rhythm; Baroque motivic imitation; tonal harmonic syntax).

Core Foundations of Counterpoint and Voice Leading

Consonance and dissonance categories are the “vertical” vocabulary of strict counterpoint: perfect consonances (unison, fifth, octave), imperfect consonances (thirds and sixths), and dissonances (seconds, sevenths, and augmented/diminished intervals; and, in strict two-voice species, the fourth because it involves the bass).
This classification is pedagogically central because it determines (i) what may occur at structurally accented positions (e.g., downbeats) and (ii) which dissonances can be tolerated only as a byproduct of a controlled melodic pattern. Species pedagogy also formalizes types of motion between voices—contrary, similar, parallel, and oblique—because the perception of independence is strongly correlated with motion type: contrary and oblique motion generally preserve separation of lines, while parallel motion (especially in perfect consonances) tends to fuse them. A baseline strict-species constraint is therefore: avoid parallel fifths and octaves; also avoid (or at least strongly limit) direct/hidden fifths and octaves (similar motion into a perfect fifth or octave), especially with leaps in the upper voice or when the outer voices move similarly into the perfect interval. Strict species begins with composing or using a well-formed cantus firmus (CF). Typical CF constraints include: modest range (often under a tenth), predominantly stepwise motion, one clear climax, avoidance of repeated motives/licks, and compensating stepwise contrary motion after larger leaps. These are not mere aesthetic preferences; they are scaffolding to ensure that the later counterpoint voice is solving vertical and horizontal problems rather than struggling against a flawed given line. In modal/Renaissance-oriented practice, cadence and melodic goal-orientation are treated as a convergence of line and mode: the final is approached by step in most voices, often through a clausula-like semitone motion created by raising the leading note where stylistically appropriate, with an important exception for Phrygian-type cadences. In tonal/common-practice practice, cadence is treated as a functional-harmonic closure (e.g., V–I authentic cadence), and the voice leading is evaluated by obvious tendency tones (especially 7̂→1̂ and 4̂→3̂ in dominant-function contexts) and by characteristic cadential embellishments (cadential 6/4 resolving 6–5 and 4–3 above 5̂ in the bass; V7 implying specific resolutions and common incomplete-or-leading-tone-drop variants). For writing in four or more parts in common-practice style, spacing, range, and doubling become explicit rule domains: a common SATB-spacing standard is ≤ octave between adjacent upper voices and ≤ twelfth between tenor and bass; voices should not cross; and the leading tone and other tendency tones (notably chordal sevenths) are not doubled under strict norms. The idea that such “rules” largely encode common practice rather than immutable acoustics is reinforced by research that tests them against corpora of Johann Sebastian Bach chorales and string quartets by Joseph Haydn and Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, finding (among other results) a strong tendency to avoid doubling leading tones/chromatic pitches and a preference for wider spacing between the two lowest parts.

Species Counterpoint Rules

The species below are presented in a consistent template. Unless noted, “strict” assumes the common pedagogical setup of two voices and a whole-note cantus firmus; “expansions” refer to later teaching practices that generalize the exercise logic toward actual repertoire textures.

Species 1

Concise definition
Species 1 (“first species,” note-against-note) aligns one note of counterpoint against each note of the cantus firmus; all vertical intervals must be consonances.

Do’s and don’ts (strict rules)

    Begin with a perfect consonance: unison, fifth, or octave. 

End with a perfect unison or octave (not a fifth).
Keep every sonority consonant (i.e., use only 3rds, 6ths, 5ths, 8ves, and unisons; treat 4ths as dissonant in two-voice species).
Avoid parallel perfect consonances (parallel fifths and octaves).
Avoid direct/hidden perfect consonances (“never enter a perfect consonance by direct motion” in the strict Fux framing).
Prefer contrary and oblique motion to similar motion to maintain independence of lines.
Use “more imperfect than perfect consonances” to avoid a hollow, overly open sound.
Keep melodic motion mostly stepwise; leap only occasionally, and do not leap a tritone or a seventh.
After a larger leap (fourth or larger), correct by step in the opposite direction (melodic compensation).
Maintain registral clarity: avoid overlapping/crossing parts in strict two-voice pedagogy.
Keep the general spacing within a reasonable span (a common strict guideline is “mostly within an octave,” with an upper bound around a twelfth in some pedagogies).
Avoid interior unisons (use unison only at the very start and end in many strict approaches).

Later pedagogical expansions (typical adjustments)

    Some instructors treat direct fifths/octaves as conditional rather than absolute: more acceptable if one voice moves by step and the sonority is not perceptually “spotlit,” especially outside the outer voices; other curriculums keep them strongly discouraged. 

In style-based Renaissance teaching, “avoid more than two perfect consonances in a row” is often treated as a soft rule rather than a hard prohibition, reflecting repertoire variability.
In tonal contexts, consonant verticality in first-species-like surfaces may be evaluated against implied harmonies (triads), not merely interval categories.

Typical melodic formulas (Species 1)

    Clausula-style ending: approach the final by step in both voices; common options include 2̂–1̂ in one voice against 7̂–1̂ (or a variant) in the other, depending on mode/voice placement. 

Consonant alternation: long stretches alternating 6ths and 3rds (with occasional perfect intervals) are a standard way to keep independence and avoid overusing open sonorities.

Permitted and forbidden intervals and motions

    Permitted harmonic intervals (strict two voices): P1, P5, P8, m/M3, m/M6. 

Forbidden harmonic intervals: all 2nds and 7ths; augmented/diminished intervals; and (in two-voice strict species) the perfect 4th.
Forbidden motions (hard): parallel 5ths and parallel 8ves; commonly also direct 5ths/8ves.
Preferred motions: contrary and oblique.

Treatment of dissonance
No dissonance is allowed in strict first species.

Cadence types and rules

    Strict/modal: end on unison or octave; penultimate sonority is typically an imperfect consonance appropriate to the voice placement (e.g., a major sixth before the final when the CF is in the lower voice, per a standard Fux-style formula). 

Tonalized reading (expansion): treat the final as a tonic arrival; avoid arriving by similar motion into the final octave in outer voices.

Examples (constructed schematic examples; pitch names are illustrative)

    Example A (CF in lower voice): CF: D–E–F–G–A–G–F–E–D; CP above: A–G–A–B–C–B–A–G–A (aiming for mostly 3rds/6ths; begin/end on perfect). 

Example B (CF in upper voice): CF: G–A–B–C–D–C–B–A–G; CP below: G–F–E–F–G–A–G–F–G (avoid 4ths against bass; keep consonances).

Common errors
Overusing perfect intervals; parallel or hidden fifths/octaves; too many similar-motion moves in the same direction; melodic lines with multiple peaks or awkward leaps; and interior unisons or voice overlap that collapses registral clarity.
Species 2

Concise definition
Species 2 places two notes of counterpoint (often half notes) against each whole note of the cantus firmus, introducing strong vs weak beats: strong beats must be consonant; weak beats may be consonant or dissonant if licensed by stepwise passing motion.

Do’s and don’ts (strict rules)

    Every downbeat (the first half note) must be consonant with the CF. 

The second half note may be dissonant only if approached and left by step (i.e., a passing dissonance on a weak beat).
Prefer consonant weak beats when possible, using them to create smooth three-consonance successions (strong–weak–strong).
Avoid parallel perfect consonances across successive downbeats; treat “downbeat-to-downbeat” as the structural skeleton.
Avoid direct motion into perfect consonances on downbeats (strict-Fux framing); treat hidden perfects as particularly dangerous when the upper voice leaps.
Do not let a dissonant weak beat be created by leap (strict): it should be “explained” as passing between consonances.
Keep the counterpoint line melodically singable; avoid static “zig-zagging” that is only there to dodge parallels.

Later pedagogical expansions (typical adjustments)
Open pedagogies explicitly name multiple consonant weak-beat patterns (beyond the simplest passing 2nd) such as substitution figures (leap then step back), skipped passing tones, interval subdivision, register shifts, and consonant neighbor figures—broadening the melodic vocabulary while preserving the strict principle that structural beats remain consonant.

Typical melodic formulas (Species 2)

    Consonant passing outline: strong consonance → step → consonant weak beat → step → consonant strong beat (often outlining a third). 

Dissonant passing on weak beat: consonant strong → step to dissonant weak → step to consonant strong (a strict template for weak-beat dissonance).

Permitted and forbidden intervals and motions

    Strong beats: consonances only (as in Species 1). 

Weak beats: may be dissonant only as stepwise passing; consonant neighbors and other consonant patterns are permitted and often recommended for variety.
Motion constraints: the same global bans (parallel 5ths/8ves; excessive direct perfects) apply, but you additionally monitor accented skeleton (downbeats) separately from embellishing weak beats.

Treatment of dissonance
Only weak-beat passing dissonance is allowed in strict second species; it must be approached and left by step.

Cadence types and rules

    Strict/modal cadence (common formula): above the CF: scale-degree pattern 5–6–8; below the CF: 5–♭3–1 (shown as a standard closing formula in a Fux-style workbook). 

Principle: keep the cadential downbeat consonant and treat any dissonance as weak-beat decoration, not structural arrival.

Examples

    Example A (dissonant passing tone on weak beat): Downbeat consonance (6th) → weak-beat 7th (passing) → next downbeat 6th; both steps in the same direction in the counterpoint voice. 

Example B (consonant neighbor): Downbeat 3rd → weak-beat 4th? (avoid if against bass) or instead neighbor within consonance (e.g., 3rd → 6th → 3rd via stepwise melodic motion depending on voicing).

Common errors
Placing dissonance on a strong beat; leaping into or out of a dissonant weak-beat tone (breaking the “passing” justification); treating every weak beat as a dissonance (monotony and instability); and forgetting that parallels are evaluated primarily from downbeat to downbeat.
Species 3

Concise definition
Species 3 places four notes of counterpoint (often quarter notes) against each whole note of the cantus firmus, introducing multiple accent levels (strong/medium/weak) and expanding the inventory of idiomatic dissonances, including neighbor figures and the nota cambiata.

Do’s and don’ts (strict rules)

    The first note of each bar (strong beat) must be consonant with the CF. 

Non-initial notes may be dissonant only if they are stepwise passing tones between consonances (the strict baseline).
Use neighbor-tone dissonance as a controlled pattern (step away and back), respecting metrical placement (typically weaker positions).
The nota cambiata (“changing tone”) is a special idiom that unusually includes a leap from a dissonance; treat it as an authorized exception, not a general license to leap from dissonance.
Maintain a coherent melodic contour despite faster rhythm: one main peak, no frantic registral toggling.
Avoid creating accented dissonance by accident: the most structurally risky positions are the downbeat and (in many conceptions) the third quarter note (“medium-strong”).
Keep the global bans: no parallel perfect consonances; avoid direct perfects; no melodic tritone/seventh leaps.
Later pedagogical expansions (typical adjustments)
Modern textbooks often classify the dissonance types explicitly: dissonant passing tone, dissonant neighbor tone, double neighbor, and cambiata (with multiple stylistic variants). In more style-historical treatments, the cambiata is tied to Renaissance practice and is differentiated into “classical” vs earlier freer forms, rather than treated as a purely abstract formula.

Typical melodic formulas (Species 3)

    Passing-chain (“fill a third”): consonant – step – dissonant – step – consonant, often filling the space of a third; can extend by chaining such gestures. 

Double neighbor: upper neighbor then lower neighbor then return (or the reverse), often used as a decorative turn-like figure in faster species.
Cambiata (one common schematic): an idiom that includes a dissonant note left by leap, then resolves by step (treated as special).

Permitted and forbidden intervals and motions

    Strong beat: consonance only. 

Other beats: dissonance only if justified by passing/neighbor/double-neighbor/cambiata syntax.
Parallel/similar motion: still monitored by structural beats; fast notes do not “erase” forbidden parallels if the structural intervals repeat in parallel.

Treatment of dissonance
Species 3 expands dissonance legality but still treats it as subordinate: dissonance is accepted mainly as an intermediate event between consonant structural tones, except for the carefully constrained cambiata-type exception.
Cadence types and rules
A common strict-species approach uses either a scale-run to the close or a cambiata-based ornamental close (with different constraints depending on whether the counterpoint is above or below the CF). The governing idea is unchanged: the cadence’s structural arrival must be consonant and approached in a way that maintains independence and avoids perfect-parallel collapse.

Examples

    Example A (double neighbor as turn): C–D–B–C in the counterpoint voice while the CF holds C (only acceptable if the vertical intervals at each point satisfy the species’ dissonance-placement rules). 

Example B (cambiata idea): a four- or five-note figure where a dissonant note is followed by a consonant leap and then by stepwise resolution (use only in the authorized formula).

Common errors
Treating “faster” as “freer”: leaping from dissonance outside cambiata patterns; accenting neighbor or passing dissonances in structurally strong positions; losing melodic integrity (multiple peaks, mechanical patterns); or hiding parallel perfects inside rapid notes.
Species 4

Concise definition
Species 4 (“fourth species,” ligature) is the suspension species: the counterpoint line is written in half notes offset by a half beat, tying weak-beat consonances across the barline to create strong-beat suspensions that resolve correctly.

Do’s and don’ts (strict rules)

    The weak-beat half note (the “preparation”) must be consonant. 

The tied note on the following downbeat may be dissonant as a suspension, but it must resolve by stepwise motion downward.
Treat the suspension as a three-part syntax: consonant preparation → dissonant suspension → consonant resolution.
Avoid “stacking” suspensions that remove the listener’s ability to hear the syncopation clearly; in multi-voice Renaissance thinking, at least one voice should articulate the beat.
Avoid certain successive dissonant ligatures into final perfect consonances (a standard strict constraint is: dissonant ligatures resolving to octave/unison in successive bars are forbidden).
If no ligature is possible, briefly revert to second-species separation, then return to ligature as soon as feasible (a common strict “escape hatch”).

Later pedagogical expansions (typical adjustments)
In tonal contexts, suspensions are treated not only as intervallic events but as nonchord-tone structures relative to harmony (e.g., 4–3 over V, 7–6 over I/ii, etc.), and their preparation/resolution is framed in terms of chord membership and tendency tones.

Typical melodic formulas (Species 4)

    Core suspension figures: 7–6 and 4–3 suspensions in the upper voice; 2–3 as an inversion of 7–6 (dissonance in the lower voice). 

Cadential suspension idea (modal/tonal bridge): a prepared tone held into the cadential arrival, then resolved stepwise at the close.

Permitted and forbidden intervals and motions

    Because one voice is tied while the other moves, pure fourth-species technique produces extensive oblique motion (a feature rather than a bug). 

The dissonance is licensed specifically by being a suspension; other dissonances are not “automatically allowed” just because the texture is syncopated.

Treatment of dissonance
Dissonance is allowed on strong beats only as a suspension that is properly prepared and resolves stepwise (typically downward); this is exactly the logic of “static note → step resolution” in later nonchord-tone taxonomies too.
Cadence types and rules
A standard strict closing formula is: if the counterpoint is above the CF, close with 7 (tied), 6, 8; if below, close with 2 (tied), ♭3, 1. This can be read as a suspension-based approach to the final perfect consonance, with the “tied dissonance” being structurally essential to the cadence’s expressive pull.

Examples

    Example A (upper-voice suspension): prepare a 6th on a weak beat, tie it so it becomes a 7th against the CF on the next downbeat, then resolve down by step to a 6th. 

Example B (2–3 in lower voice): lower voice holds into the barline to create a 2nd against the upper voice, then resolves up to a consonant 3rd (as the inversional counterpart to 7–6).

Common errors
Unprepared suspensions; “resolving” by leap; resolving in the wrong direction for the idiom; chaining suspensions that obscure tactus; and producing forbidden perfect parallels at the moment of resolution (when the ear is maximally sensitive).
Species 5

Concise definition
Species 5 (“fifth species,” florid) integrates the previous four species into a mixed-rhythm counterpoint that begins to resemble real composition: whole notes, half notes, quarters, suspensions, and limited eighth-note decoration may all appear, but dissonance remains pattern-licensed and cadence behavior remains strict.

Do’s and don’ts (strict rules)

    Treat Species 5 as a controlled mixture: you are not abandoning Species 1–4 rules; you are choosing among them musically. 

Keep structural beats consonant unless they are part of a properly prepared suspension (Species 4 logic).
Use quarters and (sparingly) eighths to decorate motions between structural tones, but maintain singability and avoid metrical congestion.
Eighth-note motion is permitted in some strict framings only on weak subdivisions (e.g., beats 2 and 4 rather than 1 and 3 in a four-beat measure), preserving the hierarchy of accent.
Use suspension embellishments that still resolve correctly; a common strict expansion describes several standard substitutions for the weak-beat part of a suspension measure while preserving the resolution.
Cadences remain strongly constrained; many strict pedagogies require using the same closing formulas as fourth species even when the preceding texture is florid.

Later pedagogical expansions (typical adjustments)
Modern curricula frame fifth species as “balancing consonance types and counterpoint types,” explicitly adding anticipation and other embellishing tones to make the surface musical while keeping the underlying logic species-driven.

Typical melodic formulas (Species 5)

    Decorated suspension: preparation (consonant) → suspension (dissonant) with an embellished weak-beat interior (e.g., subdivision or passing decoration) → correct stepwise resolution. 

Mixed-rate dialog: one voice holds while the other moves (momentary oblique motion) as a texture-balancing device, a principle later invoked in fugue pedagogy as well.

Permitted and forbidden intervals and motions
Because Species 5 is composite, interval permission is inherited from Species 1–4 plus new surface ornamentation constraints (especially for eighth notes). The global prohibitions (parallel perfect consonances; uncontrolled accented dissonance) persist.

Treatment of dissonance
Species 5 permits dissonance wherever it is licensed by a recognized pattern: passing/neighbor/escape/appoggiatura-like figures on weak positions and properly prepared suspensions on strong positions, with resolutions consistent with the dissonance type.

Cadence types and rules
Even when the texture becomes florid, the cadence is typically treated as the “strict checkpoint”: suspension-to-final closure and stepwise approach to the final remain strongly privileged, because cadence is where perceptual and structural stakes are highest.

Examples

    Example A (florid approach to cadence): quarter-note motion leading into a prepared suspension, then a clean stepwise resolution to the final perfect consonance. 

Example B (anticipation): an upcoming consonant chord tone is sounded early as a weak-beat embellishment, then becomes consonant at the chord change (a tonal/nonchord-tone framing often used later).

Common errors
Using rhythmic variety as camouflage for structural problems (parallels still matter); “decorating” without a clear structural skeleton; too many continuous fast notes (loss of phrase hierarchy); and cadences that relax strictness precisely where strictness should increase.
Beyond Species

This section treats “beyond species” as a controlled expansion along three axes: (i) style (modal Renaissance vs tonal common-practice), (ii) texture (two voices → three+ voices), and (iii) grammar (interval-licensed dissonance → chord/functional dissonance and nonchord-tone systems).
Modal and Renaissance-oriented counterpoint

Numbered rules (modal/“stile antico” emphasis)

    Treat species rules as training wheels: real 16th-century contrapuntal style is related but not identical; expect freer imitation, text-driven phrasing, and overlapping sections. 

Use imitation as a primary organizing device: points of imitation typically enter on metrically comparable positions, often starting at perfect consonances between entering voices, and may overlap between phrases.
Maintain line independence: contrary motion predominates; avoid excessive parallel writing even when the intervals are “legal,” with stylistic exceptions like fauxbourdon textures.
Keep melodic motion predominantly conjunct; approach the final by step in most parts; raise the leading note when approaching the final from below in most modal contexts except the Phrygian-type exception.
Treat dissonance types as idioms: suspensions, passing tones, and limited ornamental dissonances (including cambiata) each have characteristic preparation and resolution expectations.
In multi-voice textures, avoid “everyone syncopates”: if all parts suspend simultaneously, the suspension effect becomes unclear; ensure at least one part articulates the beat.
Cadences can coincide with overlapping imitation: it is stylistically common for a new imitative point to begin before the previous phrase fully closes (“overlap”), including around strong cadential points.
Treat vertical sonorities as emergent rather than “progressed”: the strongest constraint is sonority-by-sonority legality and line integrity, not a planned functional progression in the later tonal sense.

Examples

    A “point of imitation” where a short motive enters sequentially in multiple voices, starting at a perfect consonance between the initial pair, then continuing with small interval/rhythm adjustments and overlapping phrases, is documented as a common 16th-century opening strategy. 

Suspension sweetness and its resolution-to-consonance effect is discussed as perceptually salient in historical dissonance treatment narratives (the dissonance adding expressive “sweetness” to the consonance that follows).
Tonal counterpoint and common-practice voice-leading norms

Tonal counterpoint can be thought of as counterpoint constrained by harmonic function and by an expanded nonchord-tone system. Rameau’s fundamental-bass orientation (cadence as a fifth-descent/fourth-ascent; dissonance preparation/resolution tied to allowed bass progressions; “perfect harmony” requiring four parts) is one historical foundation for seeing harmony as the generator of voice-leading expectations.

Numbered rules (tonal/common-practice)

    Treat harmonic rhythm as structural: determine the “chord tones” (or structural consonances) and treat other tones as embellishments governed by recognized patterns. 

Resolve tendency tones reliably: in dominant-function contexts, 7̂→1̂ and 4̂→3̂ are core tendencies; in seventh chords, the chordal seventh resolves down by step and is typically approached by step or common tone.
Understand cadential 6/4 as dominant embellishment: label and voice-lead it as V(6/4) resolving 6–5 and 4–3 above 5̂ in the bass, often with bass doubling in four voices to avoid parallels.
When resolving V7 to I, expect incomplete-voicing outcomes (the I chord commonly lacks the fifth) as a normal result of proper tendency resolutions; use alternative resolutions (incomplete V7 or leading-tone drop) only when they preserve correct tendency behavior.
Avoid doubling active tones: leading tones and chordal sevenths are not doubled in strict four-voice style; this is both a common pedagogical rule and empirically consistent with corpus comparisons.
Prefer smooth voice leading over rigid doubling recipes: doubling guidance can be summarized as “double stable tones; if possible, double the root; if not, double the fifth; double the third last (with context-dependent exceptions),” which emphasizes singable lines over mechanical chord-member checklists.
Spacing and range matter in four parts: keep adjacent upper voices within an octave and tenor–bass within a twelfth; keep each voice in a plausible range; avoid voice crossing.
Treat hidden/direct perfects as context-sensitive rather than universally banned: the tradition discourages similar motion into a perfect fifth or octave (especially when it highlights the outer voices), but scholarship on pedagogy notes that such rules are often enforced more strictly in classrooms than in historical practice.
Multi-voice textures and “voice leading in four or more parts”

Guidelines for texture, spacing, and registral management

    Range norms (SATB): a common pedagogical reference range is S: C4–G5, A: G3–D5, T: C3–G4, B: F2–D4. 

Spacing: no more than an octave between adjacent upper voices; no more than a twelfth between tenor and bass; the widest spacing often belongs low in the texture (which also aligns with described spacing rules and corpus tendencies).
Voice crossing: avoid crossing voice ranges (S above A above T above B) in strict styles.
Chord completeness: triads in four voices require doubling; seventh chords typically do not double because they already contain four distinct chord tones.

Doubling conventions (practical hierarchy)

    Do not double tendency tones (leading tone; chordal sevenths; often other chromatic active tones). 

In many common contexts, doubling the root is the default; corpus-tested discussion finds typical rules broadly align with common-practice samples.
Cadential 6/4 often requires doubling the bass (5̂) in four voices to avoid parallels, reflecting its status as a dominant embellishment rather than an independent tonic chord.
Handling of nonchord tones and dissonance beyond species

A useful “translation” from species dissonance logic to tonal nonchord-tone logic is: the structural tones (often chord tones) must be consonant/appropriate to the harmony, and dissonances are allowed when they are (a) formed by a recognized motion pattern and (b) resolved according to that pattern’s norm.

Nine common nonchord-tone types by approach/leave pattern (tonal taxonomy)
Passing tone (step/step same direction), neighbor tone (step/step opposite), appoggiatura (leap/step), escape tone (step/leap opposite), anticipation (early chord tone), pedal point (static under changing harmony), suspension (static then step down), retardation (static then step up), and double neighbor.

Rules for nonchord tones (usable checklist)

    Most embellishing gestures are “three-note” patterns where the first and last notes are consonant with the bass; the middle note is often nonchordal/dissonant. 

Appoggiaturas tend to be accented and approached by leap, then resolve by step; escape tones are usually weaker and do the reverse in terms of approach/leave.
Suspensions and retardations are accented; suspensions resolve down by step, retardations up by step.
In chordal sevenths, the chordal seventh itself behaves like a controlled dissonance: approach it by step/common tone and resolve it down by step.
When multiple voices carry embellishments simultaneously, ensure the underlying harmony remains decipherable (a concern shared with Renaissance advice against universal syncopation).
Contrapuntal devices

The following devices are used as techniques of motivic/contrapuntal transformation and intensification; they are not “species,” but they interact with voice-leading constraints (especially in invertible counterpoint and imitation).

Core device definitions and constraints

    Inversion: a motive is mirrored “upside down,” with intervals reversed; inversion can be real or tonal (quality-preserving vs scale-constrained). 

Augmentation: rhythmic values are proportionally lengthened (often doubled).
Diminution: rhythmic values are proportionally shortened (often halved), or more generally replaced by shorter values.
Retrograde: the order of notes is reversed (rare in tonal styles but definable and sometimes used as a concept/device).
Stretto: overlapping entries of a subject (common as a climactic intensification in fugue practice).

Invertible counterpoint constraints (practical)
If a countersubject must work above or below a subject (e.g., invertible at the octave), avoid intervals whose inversion creates special problems (notably 5ths inverting to 4ths, and certain suspension types); prefer intervals that invert into other consonances (3rds↔6ths) and suspension pairs that invert into each other (7–6 ↔ 2–3).

Examples (verbal, style-consistent)

    Invertible countersubject example: write a countersubject that outlines consonant 3rds/6ths against the subject, reserving 5ths for non-invertible free counterpoint or for moments not intended to invert. 

Stretto example: introduce the subject in Voice 2 before Voice 1 completes it, increasing density and urgency near a cadence or climax.
Augmentation/diminution example: present a subject in longer note values in a later passage to create breadth, or in shorter values to create urgency, while re-checking voice-leading for newly created accented dissonances.
