The Architecture of Polyphony: An Exhaustive Treatise on Counterpoint and Voice Leading

The discipline of counterpoint, derived from the Latin punctus contra punctum (meaning "point against point" or "note against note"), constitutes the structural and acoustic foundation of Western polyphonic music. While the study of harmony focuses primarily on the vertical aggregation of pitches into chords and their functional progressions, counterpoint emphasizes the horizontal independence, rhythmic vitality, and linear trajectory of individual melodic lines (voices) operating simultaneously. The simultaneous sounding of these independent voices must adhere to rigorous acoustical principles to create a coherent harmonic whole, achieving a delicate equilibrium between vertical consonance and horizontal independence.  

The pedagogical transmission of contrapuntal technique is traditionally divided into two distinct historical frameworks: strict modal counterpoint and free tonal counterpoint. Strict modal counterpoint, codified in the 16th-century vocal style of Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina, treats harmony as a secondary byproduct of intersecting melodies. Conversely, 18th-century free tonal counterpoint, typified by the instrumental and vocal works of Johann Sebastian Bach, integrates linear independence with goal-oriented, functional chord progressions within the major-minor tonal system.  

The foundational method for internalizing the architectural mechanics of voice leading is "species counterpoint". Popularized by Johann Joseph Fux in his seminal 1725 treatise Gradus ad Parnassum (Steps to Parnassus), species counterpoint is a graduated, additive pedagogical system. By isolating specific rhythmic and harmonic problems through a series of increasingly complex exercises against a fixed melody (the cantus firmus), the Fuxian method strips away stylistic idiosyncrasies to teach the fundamental laws of voice independence, dissonance preparation, and acoustic resolution.  

This comprehensive report provides an exhaustive analysis of the universal rules governing species counterpoint across two, three, and four voices, traces the evolution of these principles into the 18th-century Common Practice voice-leading conventions, and details the advanced contrapuntal mechanisms required for chromatic harmony, fugal exposition, and invertible counterpoint.
Part I: Theoretical Foundations of Strict Counterpoint

Before examining the distinct species matrices, one must establish the universal axioms that govern acoustic consonance, melodic trajectory, and contrapuntal motion. The primary objective of strict counterpoint is to achieve an absolute balance between linear independence, ensuring that each voice functions as a distinct, singable melody, and vertical consonance, ensuring the simultaneous sounding of pitches is acoustically stable and pleasing.  

Consonance and Dissonance Classification

In the context of strict counterpoint, intervals are rigidly classified based on their acoustic properties, harmonic stability, and historical treatment. The European classical tradition categorizes these intervals into three functional groups.  

Classification	Intervals Included	Contrapuntal Function and Acoustic Properties
Perfect Consonances	

Perfect Unison (P1), Perfect Fifth (P5), Perfect Octave (P8).
	These intervals represent absolute acoustic rest and stability. However, their sheer perfection means they lack forward harmonic momentum. Overuse fuses the voices together, destroying linear independence.
Imperfect Consonances	

Major/Minor Thirds (M3, m3), Major/Minor Sixths (M6, m6).
	These intervals provide harmonic richness, color, and a sense of directional flow. They are the preferred vertical sonorities for the vast majority of contrapuntal motion.
Dissonances	

Major/Minor Seconds (M2, m2), Major/Minor Sevenths (M7, m7), all Augmented and Diminished intervals.
	These intervals introduce acoustic tension that demands immediate resolution. In strict counterpoint, they are forbidden on metrically accented beats unless meticulously prepared as suspensions.
 

The classification of the perfect fourth (P4) presents a unique historical and acoustical paradox. In the context of a two-voice texture, the perfect fourth is strictly classified as a dissonance. Because it involves the lowest sounding voice, it implies an unstable 6/4 chord relationship, which demands downward resolution to a third. Therefore, in two-part strict counterpoint, the perfect fourth must be treated with the exact same restrictions as a second or a seventh. It is only emancipated into a consonance in textures of three or more voices, provided the lowest voice supports the fourth with a stabilizing root (such as the interval between the third and the root in a first-inversion triad).  

Types of Contrapuntal Motion

The interaction between two independent voices is categorized by four distinct types of relative motion.  

Motion Type	Definition	Contrapuntal Application and Preference
Contrary	

The two voices move in opposite directions (one ascends while the other descends).
	

Highly preferred. Contrary motion maximizes linear independence and provides the strongest sense of separate, interacting melodies.
Oblique	

One voice remains stationary on a single pitch while the other voice moves up or down.
	

Preferred. It maintains independence while anchoring the harmony, often utilized to prepare or resolve dissonance.
Similar	

Both voices move in the same direction, but by different interval sizes (e.g., both ascend, but one by a third and the other by a step).
	

Permitted with caution. Excessive similar motion degrades independence, and it must never be used to approach a perfect consonance.
Parallel	

Both voices move in the exact same direction by the exact same interval size (e.g., parallel thirds).
	

Heavily restricted. While parallel thirds and sixths are permitted in short chains, parallel perfect intervals are strictly forbidden.
 
Universal Melodic Guidelines (The Horizontal Axis)

The foundation of species counterpoint exercises is the cantus firmus, a fixed, arhythmic melody composed entirely of whole notes. A well-formed cantus firmus, as well as any counterpoint written against it, must exhibit smoothness, directional integrity, and psychological coherence.  

According to David Huron's identified properties of melodic tendencies, melodies naturally favor pitch proximity, meaning progression by steps or small leaps is universally preferred over large, disjunct leaps. A well-constructed contrapuntal line relies heavily on step inertia, continuing in a single direction to build momentum before changing course. The melodic line must feature exactly one unique climax (the highest note) to provide architectural shape and teleological drive. The climaxes of simultaneous voices must never coincide on the same beat, as this would momentarily fuse the voices and destroy their independence.  

Furthermore, strict rules govern the use of leaps. Any leap of a perfect fourth or larger generates melodic tension that must be immediately discharged by stepwise motion in the opposite direction, a principle known as leap compensation or melodic regression. Consecutive leaps in the same direction are generally avoided; if they must occur, they should outline a consonant triad, and the second leap must be smaller than the first. Melodic leaps of an augmented interval, a diminished interval, any seventh, or any interval larger than an octave are strictly prohibited due to their unsingable nature and disruptive acoustic profile. Outlining a dissonant interval (where the outer boundaries of a melodic phrase form a tritone or seventh) is similarly forbidden.  

Part II: The Five Species of Two-Voice Counterpoint

Species counterpoint isolates specific contrapuntal challenges by adding a newly composed line against the cantus firmus according to progressively complex rhythmic and harmonic constraints. The discipline demands absolute adherence to the rules of acoustic consonance while forcing the composer to generate a compelling, independent melody.  

First Species: Note Against Note (1:1)

In first species, the counterpoint moves at the exact same rhythmic rate as the cantus firmus, resulting in a strict 1:1 ratio of whole notes. Because there is no rhythmic differentiation to distinguish the voices, linear independence must be achieved entirely through meticulous melodic trajectory and the careful curation of vertical intervals.  

Category	First Species Contrapuntal Rules
Harmonic Consonance	

Every vertical interval must be a perfect or imperfect consonance. Dissonance of any kind is strictly forbidden. Unisons may only be used on the very first or very last note; a unison in the middle of the exercise merges the voices entirely.
Opening Formulas	

The exercise must commence on a perfect consonance to establish the modal center. If the counterpoint is above the cantus firmus, it may start on the tonic (1^) forming a P1 or P8, or the dominant (5^) forming a P5. If the counterpoint is below, it must begin on the tonic (1^) forming a P1 or P8. Starting on the dominant below creates a dissonant P4 and is forbidden.
Closing Formulas	

The final interval must be a perfect unison or octave, approached by contrary stepwise motion. This cadence is known as the clausula vera. If the cantus firmus descends 2^–1^, the counterpoint must ascend 7^–1^. If the cantus firmus descends 7^–1^, the counterpoint must descend 2^–1^. The penultimate interval will therefore always be a major sixth or a minor third.
Prohibited Parallels	

Consecutive perfect intervals (P1–P1, P5–P5, P8–P8) are strictly forbidden. This includes compound intervals (e.g., P5–P12). Parallel perfect intervals instantly destroy the independence of the voices, making them sound like a single, thickened line.
Direct (Hidden) Perfects	

Moving into a perfect consonance by similar motion is forbidden. This draws undue acoustic attention to the perfect interval. Perfect consonances must be approached via contrary or oblique motion.
Imperfect Limits	

To prevent the voices from fusing into a single homophonic block, no more than three parallel thirds or three parallel sixths may be used consecutively.
Spacing and Crossing	

The distance between the voices should not exceed a perfect twelfth (an octave plus a fifth), and should ideally remain within a tenth. Voice crossing (where the upper voice dips below the lower voice) and voice overlap (where one voice leaps past the previous note held by the other) are strictly avoided to ensure the lines remain distinguishable by ear.
 
Second Species: Two Notes Against One (2:1)

Second species introduces the concept of rhythmic differentiation by placing two half notes in the counterpoint against each whole note in the cantus firmus. This rhythmic displacement establishes a metric hierarchy of strong beats (downbeats) and weak beats (upbeats), which in turn permits the introduction of the first dissonant element in Western pedagogy: the unaccented passing tone.  

Category	Second Species Contrapuntal Rules
Downbeat Consonance	

The first half note of every measure (the strong beat) must always be a perfect or imperfect consonance. Unisons should generally be avoided on downbeats.
Weak Beat Treatment	

The second half note of the measure (the weak beat) may be either consonant or dissonant. If it is consonant, it may be approached and left by leap, provided the melodic line remains singable. If it is dissonant, it is subject to severe voice-leading restrictions.
The Dissonant Passing Tone	

A dissonance in second species is exclusively permitted if it functions as a passing tone. It must fill the melodic space of a third between two consonant notes. The counterpoint must approach the dissonance by step and resolve it by continuing to step in the exact same direction. Leaping into or out of a dissonant weak beat, or changing direction upon it, is strictly forbidden.
Consecutive Downbeats	

Voice motion across the bar line from a weak beat to the subsequent downbeat follows the rigorous principles of first species. Because the structural weight of the measure falls on the downbeat, parallel perfect fifths or octaves on successive downbeats are strictly forbidden. The intervening weak-beat note does not erase the acoustic memory of the parallel perfect interval.
Consonant Melodic Figures	

Beyond the dissonant passing tone, second species utilizes several consonant idioms on the weak beat, including the consonant passing tone (outlining a perfect fourth via a step and a leap of a third), the substitution (leaping a fourth and stepping in the opposite direction to outline a third between downbeats), and the consonant neighbor tone (stepping away from and returning to the same consonant pitch).
 
Third Species: Four Notes Against One (4:1)

Third species dramatically intensifies rhythmic momentum and linear fluidity by placing four quarter notes against each whole note of the cantus firmus. The influx of weak and secondary-strong beats allows for an expanded vocabulary of dissonant melodic figures, bridging the gap between strict exercise and organic composition.  

Category	Third Species Contrapuntal Rules
Metric Hierarchy	

Beat 1 (the primary downbeat) must remain strictly consonant and must not be a unison. Beats 2, 3, and 4 may feature a mixture of consonant and dissonant intervals to promote variety. Unlike the downbeat, unisons are permitted on weak beats to facilitate smooth voice leading.
Dissonant Passing Tones	

As in second species, dissonant passing tones may fill a melodic third by stepwise motion. Due to the increased subdivision, it is permissible to have two dissonant passing tones in a row (e.g., passing sequentially through a perfect fourth and a diminished fifth) provided they do not fall on beat 1 and move consistently in a single stepwise direction.
Dissonant Neighbor Tones	

The dissonant neighbor tone, which ornaments a consonant tone by stepping one degree away and immediately returning to the original pitch, is formally introduced. It is most acoustically effective when placed on the weakest metric positions (beats 2 or 4) to ensure smoothness.
The Double Neighbor	

A four-note embellishing figure that encircles a target pitch. It occurs when beats 1 and 4 of a measure are the same consonant pitch, while beats 2 and 3 provide the notes a step higher and a step lower (e.g., C–D–B–C). Although both beats 2 and 3 are dissonant against the cantus firmus, the leap between them is acceptable because both notes structurally embellish the principal tone by step.
The Nota Cambiata	

The changing tone, or nota cambiata, is a highly specific five-note idiom that delays resolution. The classic cambiata steps down from a downbeat consonance to a dissonant beat 2, leaps down a third to a consonance on beat 3, steps up to another consonance on beat 4, and steps up again into the next measure. It is the singular exception in strict counterpoint where a dissonance is permitted to be left by leap.
 
Fourth Species: Syncopation and Suspensions

Fourth species offsets the counterpoint rhythmically by a half note, creating a pervasive syncopated effect across the barline. Both voices technically sound as whole notes, but they are metrically displaced. This species serves a singular, profound pedagogical purpose: to teach the management of accented dissonance through the mechanism of the suspension.  

Category	Fourth Species Contrapuntal Rules
The Suspension Mechanism	

A suspension figure is an accented dissonance that occurs on strong beats, governed by a strict three-part process. First, the Preparation: a weak-beat note in the counterpoint that is consonant with the cantus firmus. Second, the Suspension: the preparation is tied across the barline to the strong beat, where the movement of the cantus firmus renders it dissonant. Third, the Resolution: the suspended dissonance must resolve downward by a single step to a consonance on the subsequent weak beat.
Permitted Suspensions (Above)	

When the counterpoint lies above the cantus firmus, the permitted dissonant suspensions are 7–6, 4–3, and 9–8 (which resolves to an octave). The 7–6 and 4–3 are overwhelmingly preferred because they resolve into harmonically rich imperfect consonances. Chains of 7–6 and 4–3 suspensions define the stylistic character of the species.
Permitted Suspensions (Below)	

When the counterpoint is placed below the cantus firmus, the primary dissonant suspension is the 2–3. The lower voice holds the dissonance (a second below the cantus) and resolves downward to form a third.
Consonant Ties	

Motions such as 5–6 or 6–5 are technically consonant ties, not true dissonant suspensions, because the suspended note itself is never dissonant against the cantus firmus. They are utilized to maintain the syncopated rhythmic momentum when a true dissonant suspension is mathematically impossible to prepare.
After-Beat Parallels	

To prevent parallel fifths or octaves from forming on the weak beats (known as "after-beat" parallels), a composer must never write successive 9–8, 6–5, or 4–5 suspensions. For example, a 9–8 suspension resolves to an octave on the weak beat; chaining another 9–8 immediately after creates parallel octaves on consecutive weak beats, violating the fundamental laws of voice independence.
Breaking Species	

Because the rigid downward resolution of suspensions causes the melodic line to steadily descend, the composer must periodically leap upward from a downbeat consonance to a weak-beat consonance to keep the melody within a singable range. If neither a suspension nor a consonant tie is possible, the composer may "break species" by striking two un-tied half notes (reverting briefly to second species rules) before resuming the syncopated ties as soon as possible.
 
Fifth Species: Florid Counterpoint

Fifth species, or florid counterpoint, synthesizes the preceding four species into a fluid, lyrical line that resembles actual free composition. The challenge of florid counterpoint lies not in learning new restrictions, but in executing immense architectural pacing to balance rhythmic variety, metric stability, and melodic climax.  

Category	Fifth Species Contrapuntal Rules
Rhythmic Synthesis	

The counterpoint freely alternates among whole notes, half notes, quarter notes, and suspensions. It introduces eighth notes for the first time.
Eighth Note Constraints	

To maintain the solemnity and stability of the stile antico, eighth notes are severely restricted. They must only appear in pairs (never alone or in groups of three or four). Furthermore, they must occur strictly on weak beats—specifically filling the second or fourth quarter-note position of a 4/4 measure. They are most commonly deployed as paired passing tones or neighbor tones.
Embellished Suspensions	

The rigid, immediate downward resolution of the fourth-species suspension can be elegantly decorated in florid counterpoint. Before resolving downward by step, the suspended dissonance may be ornamented by an anticipation (dropping to the note of resolution early on the weak subdivision) or a turn (a rapid eighth-note flourish involving the upper and lower neighbors before resolving).
The Florid Cadence	

The exercise must conclude with a clausula vera. In fifth species, this demands that the penultimate measure feature an embellished suspension (typically a 7–6 above or a 2–3 below) that resolves gracefully into the contrary stepwise motion of the final octave or unison.
 
Part III: Expanding the Texture—Three and Four-Voice Species

The transition from two-voice strict counterpoint to textures involving three or four voices marks a critical juncture in music theory. As voices are added, the absolute linear independence demanded by two-voice writing must negotiate with the emergent acoustical necessity of vertical triadic completeness. The sheer density of multi-voice counterpoint necessitates slight relaxations of the strictest two-voice rules, laying the intellectual groundwork for the tonal chord structures of the 18th century.  

Harmony, Doubling, and Spacing Conventions

In three and four voices, the vertical sonorities naturally amalgamate into complete or incomplete triads. While a complete root-position triad (containing the root, third, and fifth) is the ideal vertical sonority on structural beats, incomplete triads consisting only of the root and third with a doubled pitch are perfectly acceptable and frequently utilized to preserve smooth linear motion. The open fifth (root and fifth without the third) is generally avoided as it sounds archaic and hollow in the context of richer triadic textures.  

To ensure acoustic clarity and prevent the texture from becoming muddy, specific spacing conventions are rigorously enforced. Adjacent upper voices (Soprano and Alto, or Alto and Tenor) should generally remain within an octave of one another. The bass voice, however, is fundamentally liberated from this proximity constraint; the distance between the Bass and the Tenor may safely exceed an octave, allowing the bass to serve as a resonant harmonic foundation. Voice crossing and voice overlapping remain highly restricted, as preserving the distinct registral strata of each voice is critical when the texture becomes dense.  

Modifications to Prohibited Parallels and Direct Intervals

While consecutive parallel fifths and octaves remain strictly forbidden between any two voices in the texture, the rules regarding "direct" or "hidden" perfect intervals are necessarily modified.  

Approaching a perfect fifth or octave by similar motion (a direct interval) is generally prohibited between the outer voices (Soprano and Bass) to preserve the structural frame of the harmony. However, a direct fifth or octave between the outer voices is conditionally permitted if the Soprano moves by step; this stepwise motion effectively "covers" or softens the acoustic arrival of the perfect consonance, making it acceptable to the ear. Crucially, direct perfect intervals involving the inner voices (e.g., Alto and Tenor, or Tenor and Bass) are largely permitted, as the density of the surrounding texture obscures the similar motion.  

Fauxbourdon and the Emancipation of the Perfect Fourth

A hallmark of multi-voice species counterpoint is the ability to write parallel first-inversion triads (6/3 chords). This technique, historically rooted in the Renaissance practice of fauxbourdon, allows three voices to move continuously in parallel thirds, fourths, and sixths without ever violating the prohibition against parallel fifths or octaves. It provides a lush, forward-moving texture that is mathematically impossible in two voices.  

This practice relies on a fundamental acoustical shift: in multi-voice textures, the perfect fourth is emancipated from its strict classification as a dissonance, provided it does not involve the lowest sounding voice. A perfect fourth between the Alto and Soprano is considered fully consonant, so long as the bass supports them both with a stabilizing root (e.g., forming a 6/3 chord). If the fourth occurs between the Bass and the Tenor, however, it implies a highly unstable 6/4 chord and reverts to being treated as a strict dissonance.  

Part IV: The Transition to 18th-Century Common Practice Voice Leading

The evolution from 16th-century strict modal counterpoint to 18th-century Common Practice voice leading marks a fundamental cognitive shift in Western music. In the strict species of Palestrina and Fux, harmony is a serendipitous, resultant byproduct of intersecting horizontal melodies governed by interval rules. In the 18th-century style codified by J.S. Bach and the Classical masters, counterpoint is driven by goal-oriented, functional harmony (e.g., I–IV–V–I progressions) built upon the gravitational pull of major and minor keys rather than ecclesiastical modes.  

This stylistic paradigm demands a total fusion of horizontal voice leading and vertical chordal syntax. The rules governing this synthesis are most systematically analyzed through four-part chorale texture, comprising Soprano, Alto, Tenor, and Bass (SATB).  

Foundational SATB Voice Leading Principles

Common Practice voice leading mandates that individual melodic lines be smoothly guided through functional chord progressions, prioritizing linear efficiency and the shortest possible path between chord tones.  

The primary heuristic for SATB voice leading is the retention of common tones. If a pitch is shared between two successive chords in a progression, it should ideally be retained in the exact same voice. The remaining voices must then move by step or the smallest possible leap to the nearest available tone of the new chord. Large, disjunct leaps—particularly those larger than a third—are systematically minimized in the inner voices (Alto and Tenor) to preserve a smooth, cohesive background texture. To maximize structural independence and prevent the entire chord block from shifting uniformly, the outer voices (Soprano and Bass) should utilize contrary motion whenever practical.  

Triadic Doubling Rules

Because SATB texture utilizes four voices to articulate triads that inherently contain only three distinct pitch classes (root, third, and fifth), one note must be systematically doubled to complete the texture. This doubling is not arbitrary; it is governed by the structural stability and acoustical function of the chord's inversion.  

Chord Inversion	Primary Doubling Rule	Voice Leading Exceptions and Caveats
Root Position	

Double the Bass (Root). 
	

Doubling the root reinforces the fundamental tonality of the chord. In minor or diminished chords, doubling the 3rd is occasionally acceptable to achieve smoother voice leading. The 5th is almost never doubled.
First Inversion (6/3)	

Do Not Double the Bass. Double the Soprano or inner voices.
	

The first-inversion triad is inherently lighter; doubling the bass (the 3rd) makes it ponderous. Crucial Exception: For diminished triads in first inversion (vii°6 and ii°6), the Bass (the 3rd of the chord) must be doubled. Doubling the root of a diminished triad means doubling the unstable tritone, which creates severe resolution problems. Furthermore, in chains of consecutive first-inversion chords, the composer must alternate between doubling and not doubling the bass to prevent parallel octaves.
Second Inversion (6/4)	

Double the Bass (5th). 
	

The 6/4 chord is highly unstable and functions linearly (e.g., as a cadential 6/4, a passing 6/4, or a pedal 6/4). The bass note must be strictly doubled to anchor the dissonance before it resolves downward.
 

A cardinal, inviolable rule of tonal voice leading dictates the treatment of the leading tone (scale degree 7^). Because the leading tone is the most potent tendency tone in tonal music, desperately pulling toward the tonic, it must never be doubled under any circumstance. Resolving two leading tones upward simultaneously would inevitably create forbidden parallel octaves, destroying the integrity of the counterpoint.  

Treatment of Seventh Chords

The integration of the seventh chord (a four-note sonority containing a root, third, fifth, and seventh) naturally accommodates SATB texture without the need for doubling any pitches. However, the inclusion of the chordal seventh introduces intense harmonic dissonance that requires strict, standardized resolution protocols.  

The most critical rule regarding seventh chords is that the chordal seventh (forming the interval of a 7th above the root) must always resolve downward by step. For example, in a dominant seventh (V7) chord, the chordal seventh is scale degree 4^; it is an active tendency tone that must inevitably resolve down to the stable mediant (3^) in the ensuing tonic triad. Simultaneously, the third of the V7 chord is the leading tone (7^), which is bound by its own tendency to resolve upward by step to the tonic (1^).  

This dual resolution requirement creates a specific structural problem: if a complete, root-position V7 resolves naturally to a root-position I chord, strict voice leading will result in a tonic triad that lacks a fifth (consisting of a tripled root and a third). While this is acceptable at final cadences, composers frequently circumvent this to achieve a complete tonic triad by utilizing an incomplete V7 chord—deliberately omitting the fifth of the dominant chord and doubling its root—allowing for flawless resolution into a fully voiced tonic.  

Part V: Advanced Harmonic Syntax and Chromatic Voice Leading

As Common Practice counterpoint evolved through the High Baroque and into the Classical and Romantic eras, composers increasingly relied on complex sequence chains and chromatic alterations to temporarily modulate, generate intense forward momentum, or heighten cadential tension. Voice leading these complex structures requires strict adherence to linear logic, treating chromaticism not as isolated color, but as a web of interconnected, resolving tendency tones.  

Sequential Progressions and the Circle of Fifths

Diatonic sequences construct immense structural momentum by taking a harmonic pattern and its associated voice leading and transposing it systematically up or down through the scale. The most ubiquitous and harmonically propulsive pattern in the tonal repertoire is the descending circle-of-fifths sequence.  

When voice-leading a descending circle-of-fifths sequence using root-position seventh chords, a highly specific, mechanical interlocking pattern emerges :  

    The 7-6 Suspension Chain: The fundamental engine of the sequence is the chain of sevenths resolving to thirds. The seventh of the first chord resolves down by a single step, smoothly becoming the third of the subsequent chord. Meanwhile, the third of the first chord remains stationary, tied over to become the dissonant seventh of the next chord, which must then be prepared and resolved exactly like a 4th-species suspension.   

Alternating Voicings: Because every chordal seventh is rigidly compelled to resolve downward, attempting to maintain complete (four-note) seventh chords throughout a root-position sequence inevitably forces all upper voices to descend, resulting in forbidden parallel fifths. Therefore, composers must strictly alternate between complete seventh chords and incomplete seventh chords (where the fifth is omitted and the root is doubled).  

Inverted Seventh Chains: If the sequence utilizes alternating inverted seventh chords rather than root-position chords (e.g., alternating between 6/5 and 4/2 inversions), the voice leading becomes flawlessly smooth without the need to omit notes. Two voices simply remain stationary on common tones (shown with ties), while the other two voices descend by step, locking the harmony in a descending spiral.  

Voice Leading Chromatic Harmonies

When introducing pitches outside the diatonic key signature—such as secondary dominants, borrowed chords via modal mixture, or augmented sixth chords—counterpoint becomes the critical mechanism for integrating these foreign, dissonant elements smoothly.  

Secondary Dominants (Applied Chords):
When utilizing a secondary dominant (e.g., a V7/IV chord used to temporarily tonicize the subdominant), the chromatically altered note typically serves as a temporary leading tone to the new target chord. This altered note functions exactly like a diatonic leading tone and must resolve upward by a half-step. Simultaneously, the chordal seventh of the applied chord must resolve downward by step. When voice leading involves chromatic alterations of a single scale degree (e.g., moving from F♮ to F♯), this chromatic line should remain entirely within the same voice part to prevent the jarring acoustic clash known as a cross-relation.  

The Neapolitan Sixth (♭II6):
The Neapolitan chord is a highly expressive chromatic predominant built on the lowered second scale degree (♭2^), typically appearing in first inversion.  

    Doubling: Because both the chromatic root (♭2^) and the fifth (♭6^ in minor) are highly active tendency tones pulling downward, doubling them is disastrous. Therefore, the bass note of the first-inversion chord (scale degree 4^, the third of the chord) is strictly doubled.   

Voice Leading Resolution: The defining melodic characteristic of the Neapolitan is the intense gravitational descent of the altered root (♭2^). It resolves downward by a diminished third directly to the leading tone (7^) of the ensuing dominant chord, violently bypassing the tonic pitch entirely.  

Augmented Sixth Chords (Italian, French, German):
Augmented sixth chords are intense chromatic predominants built upon the structural interval of an augmented sixth. This interval is formed between the flattened sixth scale degree (♭6^) placed in the bass, and the raised fourth scale degree (♯4^) placed in an upper voice.  

    The Wedge Resolution: The voice leading of the defining augmented sixth interval is immutable: it must resolve outward by contrary half-steps to an octave on the dominant pitch (5^). The bass voice (♭6^) descends a half-step to 5^, while the upper voice (♯4^) ascends a half-step to 5^. This dual half-step motion toward the dominant creates immense harmonic propulsion.   

The German 6th Parallel Fifths Problem: The German Augmented Sixth chord (containing ♭6^, 1^, ♭3^, and ♯4^) poses a unique and notorious contrapuntal hazard. If the German 6th resolves directly to a root-position V chord, the voice leading inevitably results in parallel perfect fifths between the bass (♭6^ dropping to 5^) and the tenor or alto (♭3^ dropping to 2^). To circumvent this theoretical error while preserving the rich sonority of the chord, composers routinely resolve the German 6th to a cadential 6/4 chord; the 1^ and ♭3^ remain stationary as common tones, delaying the arrival of the dominant fifth and allowing pristine, error-free voice leading.  

Part VI: Imitative Polyphony, Fugue, and Invertible Counterpoint

The absolute zenith of 18th-century polyphonic complexity is found in imitative counterpoint and the fugue. A fugue is not merely a form, but a rigorous contrapuntal procedure wherein a single melodic subject is stated independently and sequentially across all voices. It generates an intensely interwoven texture of overlapping lines without ever compromising the underlying harmonic syntax.  

The Fugal Exposition: Subjects and Answers

A fugue opens with an "exposition," beginning with an unaccompanied statement of the main theme, known as the Subject, typically in the tonic key. As the first voice concludes the subject, a second voice enters with the Answer—the subject transposed to the dominant key.  

The counterpoint governing the intervallic construction of the answer is highly regulated to preserve the psychological stability of the home key during the delicate opening measures of the exposition. Answers are divided into two strict categories :  

    Real Answer: An exact, interval-for-interval transposition of the subject into the dominant key. This is utilized when the subject does not emphasize the dominant pitch or imply a modulation.   

Tonal Answer: A meticulously modified transposition of the subject. A tonal answer is required if the subject opens with a prominent leap from the tonic to the dominant (1^ to 5^), or if the subject inherently modulates to the dominant by its conclusion. Historically rooted in the Renaissance necessity to answer an authentic mode with its plagal counterpart, the tonal answer prevents the fugue from jarringly leaping out of the established tonality. The dominant leap in the subject is deliberately answered by a tonic leap (5^ to 1^), fundamentally altering the intervallic structure (e.g., answering a perfect fifth with a perfect fourth) to maintain harmonic cohesion and keep the exposition anchored in the tonic.  

The Countersubject and Invertible Counterpoint

While the second voice states the answer, the first voice continues with newly composed material. If this new contrapuntal line is designed to consistently accompany the subject every time it appears throughout the fugue, it is codified as a Countersubject.  

Because the subject and countersubject will inevitably appear in multiple voices across varying registers—sometimes with the subject in the Soprano and the countersubject in the Bass, and later vice versa—they must be written utilizing the rigorous arithmetic of Invertible (Double) Counterpoint. Invertible counterpoint is designed such that either melody can serve as the harmonic bass (the lowest sounding voice) without generating forbidden dissonances or illicit parallel motions.  

Invertible Counterpoint at the Octave (or 15th)

When two voices are designed to invert at the octave, one voice is transposed up or down an octave to swap registral positions. The intervals invert mathematically (the sum of the original and inverted interval always equals 9).  

    Unisons invert to Octaves (1 ↔ 8)

    Seconds invert to Sevenths (2 ↔ 7)

    Thirds invert to Sixths (3 ↔ 6)

The Problem of the Perfect Fifth:
In inversion at the octave, the perfect fifth inverts to a perfect fourth (5 ↔ 4). Because the perfect fourth is strictly considered a dissonance when it involves the lowest voice, any perfect fifth used in the original counterpoint will instantly become an illicit, dissonant 6/4 relationship upon inversion. Therefore, the primary and most restrictive rule of double counterpoint at the octave is that the perfect fifth must be treated as a dissonance, utilized solely as a passing tone or a meticulously prepared suspension. Furthermore, standard 4-3 suspensions cannot be used, as they invert to 5-6 motions, completely negating the necessary dissonance. Instead, composers strictly utilize 7-6 and 2-3 suspensions, which uniquely invert into one another smoothly.  

Invertible Counterpoint at the Tenth and Twelfth

Though considerably less common and vastly more difficult to compose, subjects and countersubjects may be designed to invert at intervals other than the octave, yielding radically different harmonic implications upon inversion.  

Inversion Matrix	Arithmetic Mechanisms and Voice Leading Restrictions
Inversion at the Tenth	

One voice is transposed by an octave, while the other is transposed by a tenth. Because parallel thirds invert to parallel octaves (3 ↔ 8) and parallel sixths invert to parallel fifths (6 ↔ 5), consecutive thirds and sixths are strictly forbidden. Composers must use contrary motion almost exclusively to prevent catastrophic parallel perfect intervals from emerging upon inversion.
Inversion at the Twelfth	

One voice moves an octave while the other moves a twelfth. In this highly specialized matrix, the interval of a sixth inverts to a seventh (6 ↔ 7). Thus, the major and minor sixth, typically the most stable and heavily relied-upon imperfect consonances in standard counterpoint, transform into profound dissonances. Sixths must therefore be completely avoided on structural beats, or strictly prepared and resolved as if they were suspensions or passing tones.
 
Stretto, Augmentation, and Diminution

Fugal development often relies on the advanced contrapuntal manipulation of the subject itself to build architectural tension prior to the final cadence.  

The most dramatic of these devices is Stretto, which involves the overlapping, canonical entry of the subject in multiple voices before the first statement has even concluded. Composing for stretto requires that the subject be carefully designed from its inception to harmonize with its own staggered echo, requiring meticulous calculation of consonant leaps and stepwise momentum to avoid crushing dissonances.  

Additionally, a subject may be stated in Augmentation (where the rhythmic values are doubled, causing the theme to move twice as slow, often utilized in the bass like a monumental cantus firmus) or Diminution (where the values are halved, moving twice as fast). Counterpoints written against augmented subjects require florid, continuous passing motion to sustain harmonic interest and rhythmic vitality over the extended, sustained durations.  

The synthesis of these hyper-rational, mathematically rigorous operations—whether through species abstraction, SATB voice leading, or the labyrinthine matrices of invertible fugal counterpoint—represents the ultimate triumph of the compositional intellect. By subjecting melodic invention to the unforgiving acoustics of consonance and dissonance, the rules of counterpoint paradoxically liberate the composer to construct the most complex, enduring polyphonic architectures in Western music.
